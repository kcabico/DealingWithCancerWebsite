Welcome to our Website!!!

As you can see, there are five of us that make up the children in the family. Ashley, the oldest at 19, is a sophomore at USC studying structural engineering. Katelyn, on the left in the picture, is 13 years old and is in the 8th grade. Mackenzie, standing next to Katelyn, is 7 years old and is in the 2nd grade. The twins, Sean and Ryan, are 5 years old and are attending a special needs kindergarten class in the Fall. Mackenzie and the twins were adopted through the Arizona State Foster System. We all love our Dad very much.

In February of 2008, our dad was diagnosed with stage 4 follicular non-Hodgkin's lymphoma. He began chemotherapy treatments on Valentine's Day which were repeated every 3 weeks. He doesn't look sick. He had no symptoms.   But in April, he began to get pain in his right hip. The doctors said that the cancer has spread into the bone in his hip. So they have changed the chemotherapy by adding an additional drug that is stronger. That chemotherapy did not work. We  transferred his care to MD Anderson Cancer Center in Texas. The doctors changed to 2 different types of chemotherapy with the last being the strongest they could give him. They say that there is only one real cure for this type of cancer and that is to have a bone marrow transplant. After reading the literature and statistics, we were astounded to find that of the more than 7 million adult registered donors, only 9000 of them fall under the Hawaiian and Pacific Islander category. To put that into greater perspective, that’s one tenth of one percent. Here is my analogy – Let’s say your employer pays you $100 an hour but you were taxed on that money and your take-home pay was 10 cents per hour. How many hours would you have to work to make a living?

The goal of this website is to find a bone marrow match for our Dad. More importantly, the goal of this web site is to get more people registered as bone marrow donors and find matches for others in need of stem cell transplantation. In order to do that, we need to educate the public on the need for people to register on the national bone marrow donor registry, especially minorities.
