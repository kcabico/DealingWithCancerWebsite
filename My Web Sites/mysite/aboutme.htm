This is a little autobiography about our Dad, Art Cabico.

My name is Jacinto Arthur Cabico and the following is a short autobiography I wrote for NMDP at the beginning of my journey to find a cure.

My father came to the United States from the Philippines in the early 1920’s. His first stop was the Hawaiian Islands where he worked as a farm hand. There, he raised money for his brothers and cousins to immigrate from the Philippines. When America joined the second world war, he joined the U.S. Navy and served until the end of the war. He earned his citizenship by fighting in the war and was in Hawaii when Hawaii was awarded statehood. My father met my mother in the Philippines in 1962. He was visiting for his brother’s wedding. She was the sister of his brother’s new wife. They were then married in 1963 which as the story goes, two brothers married two sisters. My father was 59 and my mother was 24 when they wed. I was born in 1964 and grew up in California’s famed San Fernando Valley. I was born Jacinto Arthur Cabico. Of course, for my entire life no one could pronounce my first name correctly (let alone my last name) so everyone has known me by my middle name - Art. For half my childhood two families lived together under the same roof – my Uncle, Aunt, two cousins, mom, dad, and myself. A couple of years later, my mother’s parents came to live with us also.

So I grew up and spent my formative and high school years in the late 1970’s and early 1980’s in Southern California. I was happy in 1982 to be accepted to the University of Notre Dame’s Undergraduate program where I spent 4 years in Indiana. After graduating in 1986, I returned to Southern California where I worked in the medical field for a number years as a manager in a large Diagnostic Imaging firm. In 1987, I entered my first marriage and in 1989 my daughter, Ashley, was born in Torrance, CA.

In 1992, I was employed at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center as a research associate in Nuclear Medicine Imaging. This year also saw the end of my first marriage which saw many struggles, but I still maintained active part of Ashley’s life. While at CSMC, I met and fell in love with Robin. We were married in April of 1994 and similarly to my parents, our daughter, Katelyn, was born a little over 1 year later. Katelyn’s delivery and birth was met with many struggles. Her birth occurred two weeks after her due date and was an induced delivery which then turned into an emergency c-section procedure because of fetal distress. After her entry into the world, which is supposed to be one of the most joyous times in a parent’s life, I was approached by the obstetrics doctor who informed me that Robin had lost almost all of her blood on the operating table and all life-saving procedures at their disposal had been attempted ending with a radical hysterectomy. I was told to inform all of our immediate family members to come to the hospital because Robin would not make it through the night. But Robin is a fighter and did not give up and after two weeks in Intensive Care, surprised everyone by using every ounce of strength to combat renal failure and liver failure. She was discharged from the hospital after three weeks.

Needless to say, after such a life changing event, we made the decision together to leave Southern California and move to Phoenix, Arizona to be near Robin’s family. In late 1995, we said goodbye to the west coast and moved to the desert. We made our home in Mesa, Arizona. In the beginning, we attempted to have more children by employing surrogacy. After multiple treatments and the complete exhaustion of our retirement funds, we were unable to have more children. The stress and challenges of such an ordeal tend to test a marriage and a family’s ability to sustain itself in adversity. But by the grace of God and our faith we endured and I believe developed an even stronger bond.

In the late 1990’s, we decided that God’s plan for was of a greater purpose and that we really were not meant to have more biological children. Instead, we were to help children as foster parents and so we took an active role and became certified to foster special needs children through an agency called Aid to Adoption of Special-needs Kids. We saw many children come through our home as we provided a place for these kids to stay and our daughter learned many lessons in life through this special service. Many of these children we would have adopted in a heartbeat but with all of our advocacy and good intentions, they were never permanently placed with us.

After a while, one begins to doubt our purpose in His eyes and as children came and went through our home, we became discouraged. It was near the end of our foster care license that we decided it was just not meant to be and we would not be adopting more children. We gave up the intense fight and decided to leave the path we had chosen in His hands. We were called by the agency that a woman was looking for a family to adopt her unborn daughter and had come to the agency for assistance. We were told that twenty other families were placed in front of this mother but that if we liked, our family would be added to the list of potential adoptive families. All the odds were against us being picked but we decided to put our faith to the test and leave it in God’s hands. Our family was chosen and two weeks later, we received the call that we had a new daughter, Mackenzie, to add to our family. We had increased our family by one precious little child. We decided to renew our foster care license and for the next two years continued to take in special needs children through our home.

In January of 2003, we were called by the agency and asked if we could take two premature baby boys who needed a home immediately. We were told that they tested positive for various controlled substances which was the cause for their 10 week premature birth. They came into this world at just over 2 pounds and came into our lives at about 4 pounds each. They had numerous challenges and the state provided such services as Early Intervention therapy, physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy just to name a few. The beginning of their lives consisted of a variety of many doctors’ visits throughout the week and Robin juggling a 6-year-old, an 18-month-old and now twin boys. The agency informed us from the beginning that this was a temporary placement in our home just as all the others and that a permanent home would be found for the twins in 6-8 weeks.

In August of 2007, we adopted the twins who were then 4-and-a-half years old. Our family had exploded to 5 children in all. Our family had been through many struggles and admittedly many challenges that have arisen due to the nature raising these special needs twins. But they are our sons, and because of my family’s history of longevity, we believe that our family is the best place for these children and that we would fight to keep them all because they were members of  “Our Family.”

Life changes in the blink of an eye as they say and in January of 2008, as the result of a physical exam needed to renew our foster care license, fluid was discovered in my lungs. At first, because of my Filipino background, Valley Fever had to be ruled out. After ruling this out, the only thing left was some sort of lymphoma. Almost weekly, I had to go in and have 2 liters (about the size of a large soda bottle) of fluid were removed from my lungs. I also had a barrage of tests performed and they all pointed to cancer. After a last set of outpatient scans, it was decided that I needed a surgeon to biopsy the lymph node in my abdomen to get a definitive diagnosis. We felt that time was running out but we were at a loss as to how to expedite the process. Over the weekend I developed severe pain in back which I attributed to a kidney stone that was seen on a previous scan. We went to the emergency department where they diagnosed me as having passed a kidney stone but cancer of the urinary system could not be ruled out. I was sent home with pain medication and orders to follow up with my doctor on Monday. However, Sunday morning arrived and so did the intense pain once again in my back. After the church service, it was unbearable and so after speaking the on-call doctor, we decided to go to the hospital and request to be admitted.

Following an 8 hour ordeal in the emergency room, I was finally admitted with a diagnosis of renal failure. My kidneys were shutting down and we did not know the reason. From the start of this process, Robin has been my strength and my biggest advocate. Without her, I would have succumbed to depression from the shear stress of what I am undergoing. She pushed the doctors to find a resolution to my illness and come to a final diagnosis that we could work with. After a final diagnosis of Stage 4 non-Hodgkin’s follicular lymphoma, I began chemotherapy in the hospital. I have been through 11 rounds so far. The last hurdle involved the growth of the cancer into my right hip and the new involvement of bone.

This brings us to today. The doctors tell me that because the pain and the appearance of cancer cells in my right hip, the type of cancer is likely aggressive and not the slow-growing type we had originally thought. And although the lymph nodes throughout my body appear to be responding to the chemotherapy, the biopsy of the bone in my right hip indicates a more aggressive lymphoma. I have seen a specialist in bone marrow transplants located in Phoenix and it is her opinion based on years of experience that my best chance to beat this disease is to have a bone marrow transplant from a donor. After delays in going forward, we decided to transfer care to MD Anderson Cancer Center in Houston, Texas. U.S. News and World Report has ranked this hospital number one in the nation for treating cancer. We are currently on the highest chemotherapy that they can put my body through in an attempt to get the cancer into remission. As we stated earlier, the only way to cure this disease is for me to have a bone marrow transplant from an unrelated donor. We have already tested the closest possible related donors but unfortunately, they do not match on enough significant markers.

Quite an interesting twist of events. In 1996, while working at Good Samaritan Medical Center in Phoenix, I registered as a bone marrow donor with the National Bone Marrow Donor program. After reading the literature and statistics, I was astounded to find that of the more than 7 million adult registered donors, only 9000 of them fall under the Hawaiian and Pacific Islander category. To put that into greater perspective, that’s one tenth of one percent. Here is my analogy – Let’s say your employer pays you $100 an hour but you were taxed on that money and your take-home pay was 10 cents per hour. How many hours would you have to work to make a living? So I registered knowing that my donation could make a difference and save someone’s life. Now 12 years later, I find myself on the other side of the coin – looking for someone who can save mine. Because statistically, the best match in bone marrow donation would be someone who is Filipino but if you look at the percentages of Pacific Islanders on the registry, my odds are not very good. So to tip the scales in my favor, I am going to do whatever I can to help educate the many Pacific Islanders (Filipinos in particular) out there that they can make a difference by registering as a bone marrow donor. And by doing so, we can change the statistics and save many more lives.

Well-renowned professor at Carnegie Mellon University and author of the book, The Last Lecture, Randy Pausch said, “We cannot change the cards we are dealt, just how we play the hand.” This is my approach to life as it now involves cancer. There is no one to blame and certainly not God.  Is it unfair that I have cancer? No – cancer does not choose from economic, political or financial demographics. It is simply just unlucky - just like the hand you are dealt in poker. But even a bad hand in poker can win if one just plays the cards right.
